Endangered & Endemic Species in India

An endangered species is a plant or animal species that is at risk of becoming extinct in the near future. The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) defines an endangered species as one that has a very high risk of extinction in the wild, while a critically endangered species is one that is facing an extremely high risk of extinction. A species may be designated as endangered for a variety of reasons, including habitat destruction, overhunting, introduction of non-native species, pollution, and climate change.

An endemic species is a plant or animal species that is found only in a particular geographic region and nowhere else in the world. These species are uniquely adapted to their specific environment and are often highly specialized, which makes them particularly vulnerable to habitat loss and other threats. Endemic species can occur at any taxonomic level, from individual populations to entire genera or families, and they can be found in a wide range of habitats, including forests, deserts, and islands.

bengal tiger



The Bengal tiger, also known as the Royal Bengal tiger, is a tiger subspecies native to the Indian subcontinent. It is one of the most iconic and majestic animals in the world, with its distinctive orange coat and black stripes.


Habitat: The Bengal tiger's habitat is primarily in India, but they can also be found in other countries such as Bangladesh, Bhutan, and Nepal. They prefer to live in dense forests, grasslands, and wetlands where they can hunt their prey.


Behaviour: Bengal tigers are solitary animals, and each individual has its own territory. They are known for their strength, agility, and intelligence, and are considered to be apex predators. They are also excellent swimmers and are known to hunt in water.


Conservation Status: The Bengal tiger is listed as endangered by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) due to habitat loss, poaching, and human-tiger conflict. Conservation efforts have been made to protect the species, including the creation of national parks and the enforcement of stricter wildlife protection laws.

Snow Leopard



The snow leopard (Panthera uncia) is a large, solitary cat native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia, including the Himalayas, Tibet, and the ltai Mountains. They are known for their distinctive white-gray fur with black spots and their powerful build, adapted to the harsh conditions of their high-altitude habitats.


Snow leopards are perfectly adapted to their cold, mountainous environments. They have long, thick fur that insulates them from the cold, and large, furry paws that help them walk on snow and ice.


sSnow leopards are carnivorous and primarily feed on wild sheep, goats, and other ungulates. They are also known to eat small mammals, such as marmots and pikas, and occasionally birds.


Efforts are underway to conserve snow leopards and their habitats. Conservation initiatives include anti-poaching patrols, community education programs, and the establishment of protected areas.

Asian Elephant



Asian elephants (Elephas maximus) are majestic and intelligent mammals that are native to Asia, specifically found in 13 countries including India, Sri Lanka, Thailand, and Malaysia.

They are slightly smaller than their African counterparts, with a shoulder height of up to 3 meters and a weight of up to 5,500 kg. Asian elephants have distinctive features such as smaller ears, a more arched back, and a single "finger" at the tip of their trunk, which they use for grasping objects.

These elephants are herbivores and feed on a variety of vegetation, including grasses, leaves, bark, and fruits. They are social animals, living in groups led by a dominant female, and communicate with each other using various vocalizations and body language.

Unfortunately, Asian elephants are listed as endangered on the IUCN Red List due to habitat loss, poaching, and human-elephant conflict. Conservation efforts are underway to protect these magnificent animals and their habitats.

Red Panda



The red panda is a small arboreal mammal found in the forests of India, Nepal, Bhutan, and the northern mountains of Myanmar and southern China.

It thrives best at 2,200-4,800m in mixed deciduous and conifer forests with dense understories of bamboo, though red panda evidences have also been found at 1800m.

In India, this elusive species is found in Sikkim, Arunachal Pradesh, Darjeeling and Kalimpong districts of West Bengal. It is the state animal of Sikkim.

Endemic Species

The Asiatic Lion and the Gir Forest The Asiatic Lion, commonly known as the Indian Lion, is endemic to only the Gir Forest National Park in Gujarat and the areas immediately around it. These animals are considered to be on the verge of extinction. These are two of India five big cats; the others are the Indian Leopard and the Bengal Tiger. India is home to a total of five large cats

Kashmir Stag, Kashmir Valley The Dachigam National Park in the Kashmir Valley and the Chamba district in Himachal Pradesh are home to the Hangul, another name for the Kashmir Stag. Both of these areas are located in Himachal Pradesh.

Purple Frog, Western Ghats The only place on Earth where you can find the purple frog, also called the Pignose frog, is in the rainforests of India western ghats. It burrows underground for most of its existence.

Sangai Deer, Loktak Lake In addition to its other name, the Brow Antlered Deer, this species can only be found in Manipur Keibul Lamjao National Park. This park is a marshy wetland and it is situated in the southern portions of Loktak lake.

Nilgiri Tahr


The species is locally known as Varaiaadu. It was designated as the State animal of Tamil Nadu in recognition of its ecological and cultural significance.

It has become locally extinct in around 14% of its traditional shola forest-grassland habitat. Population: It is estimated that there are 3,122 Nilgiri Tahrs in the wild. October 7 will be celebrated as Nilgiri Tahr Day in honour of E.R.C. Davidar.


Habits and Lifestyle
Nilgiri tahrs are social animals and live in herds that may consist of 6 individuals or as large as 100-150; these herds may be mixed or contain only males or only adult females and their young. Nilgiri tahrs are active between dawn and dusk. Nilgiri tahrs have very acute eyesight and are able to spot danger at a far distance; with any sight of a danger, they produce an alarm call that sounds as a snort or whistle.


Diet and Nutrition
Nilgiri tahrs are herbivores (graminivores, folivores). They feed on various grasses, shrubs, leaves, and forbs.
